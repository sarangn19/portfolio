Overview

The problem hiding in plain sight

Young professionals (22–30) often struggle to reconcile their available balance with their actual savings. Fixed EMIs, variable daily expenses, and fragmented financial tools create a false sense of financial security.

Current financial apps act as digital passbooks — they record the past. They do not help users predict the future. This leads to "EMI Blindness," where users commit to new installments without understanding the long-term erosion of their monthly liquidity.

"If my account shows ₹20k left, I assume I'm doing okay."

User interview, 25-year-old software engineer

Secondary Research · Competitor Analysis

What existing tools get right — and miss

Existing financial apps in India — Walnut, CRED, Money Manager, ET Money, and Mint — were analyzed across expense tracking, EMI visibility, budgeting, and financial insights.

What works
Automatic expense tracking
Apps like Walnut detect expenses from SMS alerts — widely used in India where users do not connect bank accounts directly.
What works
Credit card management
CRED handles bill reminders, rewards, and payment tracking across multiple credit cards effectively.
Gap 01
No real EMI visibility
Most apps do not show how phone EMIs, bike loans, education loans, and credit card EMIs combine to affect actual monthly savings.
Core gap
Past-focused, not future-focused
Every major app tracks what already happened. None help users understand what will happen if they take on a new financial commitment.

Qualitative Research · 30 User Interviews

Four patterns that kept coming up

01
Bank balance does not equal real savings
Users rely on remaining bank balance to judge financial health rather than calculating savings after EMIs and expenses.
"If my account shows ₹20k left, I assume I'm doing okay."
02
EMI decisions are often impulsive
Participants decide on new EMIs based on current salary or available balance — not long-term affordability. The monthly amount feels manageable in isolation.
"If the monthly EMI looks small, I usually just take it."
03
Small UPI transactions are invisible
Frequent small payments — food delivery, subscriptions, UPI transfers — are nearly impossible to track manually. They accumulate silently and cause end-of-month confusion.
"Money disappears through small UPI payments."
04
Tools show the past, not the future
Most financial apps show spending history. Users want help understanding what happens next — how a new EMI will affect next month, not just what they spent last month.
Affinity Mapping

Clustering the data into themes

Theme 01
Lack of financial visibility
Users can see transactions but still feel unsure about how much they can safely spend. Bank balance is used as a proxy for financial health.
Theme 02
EMI overload
Multiple EMIs evaluated individually feel affordable. Combined, they consume a disproportionate share of income — often more than half.
Theme 03
Inconsistent tracking
Users start tracking with apps or spreadsheets but stop after a few days. Financial data becomes incomplete and unreliable quickly.
Theme 04
Hidden recurring expenses
Subscriptions and small daily UPI transactions accumulate unnoticed. Users feel like money disappears without a clear cause.

Problem Statement

The core problem, clearly defined

Young professionals frequently check their bank balance but this number creates a false sense of financial security — it does not reflect upcoming EMIs, recurring bills, or daily spending patterns.

This gap leads to "EMI Blindness" — users commit to multiple installments without a clear view of their true disposable income or savings.

How Might We

Reframing problems as design opportunities

HMW help users clearly understand their actual savings after paying EMIs and monthly expenses?
HMW show users how much money they can safely spend without affecting their financial stability?
HMW help users understand the long-term impact of taking new EMIs before committing to them?
HMW automatically track small daily transactions like UPI payments and subscriptions?
HMW provide a unified view of income, expenses, EMIs, and savings in one dashboard?

Product Hypothesis

The bet this product is making

If young professionals are given a financial tool that clearly shows their actual savings after EMIs, bills, and daily expenses — along with a Safe-to-Spend balance and a simulator for new EMI impact — they will make better financial decisions and avoid committing to installments that erode their monthly liquidity.

This shifts financial tools from transaction recorders to decision-support systems. The goal is not to track the past more accurately — it is to help users understand the future before they commit to it.
