Overview

The problem hiding in plain sight

EMI options are everywhere in Indian ecommerce — but most users do not understand what they are agreeing to. The monthly amount looks small. The total cost is buried. The interest is invisible.

"I thought I was paying 999 per month. I did not realise I would pay 14,000 in total."

User interview, 24-year-old first-time EMI user
The Problem

EMI blindness is a design problem

Current EMI interfaces are optimised for conversion, not comprehension. They highlight the monthly amount while hiding the total cost, interest rate, and processing fees.

Research

What users actually experience

Interviews and usability tests with 8 participants aged 22–38, all of whom had used EMI at least once.

Pattern 01
Monthly anchoring
Users fixate on the monthly amount and ignore the tenure. 833/month feels affordable. 10,000 total does not.
Pattern 02
No Cost confusion
Most users believe No Cost EMI means they pay the product price. In reality, interest is often pre-loaded into the MRP.
Pattern 03
Hidden fee blindness
Processing fees appear only at the final payment screen. By then, users feel committed and rarely go back.
Core insight
Trust breaks at the end, not the beginning
Users do not feel deceived when they select an EMI. They feel deceived when they see the final bill.
User Scenario

The moment trust breaks

Priya, 26 — Software Engineer, Bengaluru
Buying a phone on a shopping app
Current experience

Sees ₹749/month × 12 prominently. Taps proceed. Reaches payment screen. Sees ₹9,588 total + ₹299 fee. Feels tricked. Completes purchase but leaves a 2-star review.

Redesigned experience

Sees ₹749/month × 12 = ₹8,988 total upfront. Understands the ₹299 fee before proceeding. Makes an informed choice with confidence.

Design Decisions

What I changed and why

Rejected
Show only monthly amount
Optimises for conversion but creates post-purchase regret.
Chosen
Show monthly and total cost together, always
Both numbers visible at the same time. Trust is built before commitment, not broken after.
Rejected
Reveal processing fee only at payment screen
Feels like a bait-and-switch. Increases abandonment and negative sentiment.
Chosen
Show all fees inline during EMI selection
No surprises. Users who proceed have genuinely consented.
Rejected
Use No Cost EMI label without explanation
Misleading for 41% of users. Creates false expectations.
Chosen
Explain No Cost EMI with a one-line tooltip
A single honest line that sets the right expectation without killing conversion.

Risks and Tradeoffs

What this design gives up

01
Short-term conversion may dip
Showing full cost upfront may reduce impulse EMI selections. The bet is that higher-quality conversions offset this over time.
02
More information means more cognitive load
Mitigated through progressive disclosure — key numbers prominent, details available on tap.
03
Business stakeholder resistance
The counter-argument: trust drives repeat purchase, and repeat purchase drives LTV.

Reflection

What this taught me

The most interesting design problems are not about making things look better. They are about making things work honestly. EMI blindness exists because it is profitable in the short term. Designing against it means arguing that trust is a better long-term asset than conversion rate.

This project also taught me that edge cases are where design maturity shows. Anyone can design the happy path. The real work is in the bank eligibility failure, the coupon applied mid-flow, the user who changes their mind at the last screen.
