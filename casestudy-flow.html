Designing Against
EMI Blindness

How do you help users make smarter financial decisions when the product is designed to obscure the true cost? A deep dive into redesigning the EMI experience for clarity, trust, and informed consent.

DomainFintech - Consumer Finance
TypeUX Research + Product Design
FocusFinancial Clarity - Trust Design
ToolsFigma - FigJam - Research
Overview

The problem hiding in plain sight

EMI options are everywhere in Indian ecommerce and fintech but most users do not actually understand what they are agreeing to. The monthly amount looks small. The total cost is buried. The interest is invisible.

This case study explores how to redesign the EMI selection and checkout experience to give users genuine financial clarity without removing the product's commercial viability.

"I thought I was paying 999 per month. I did not realise I would pay 14,000 in total."

User interview, 24-year-old first-time EMI user
The Problem

EMI blindness is a design problem

Current EMI interfaces are optimised for conversion, not comprehension. They highlight the monthly amount while hiding the total cost, interest rate, and processing fees.

Research

What users actually experience

Interviews and usability tests with 8 participants across age groups 22 to 38, all of whom had used EMI at least once. The patterns were consistent.

Pattern 01
Monthly anchoring
Users fixate on the monthly amount and mentally ignore the tenure. 833 per month feels affordable. 10,000 total does not.
Pattern 02
No Cost confusion
Most users believe No Cost EMI means they pay the same as the product price. In reality, the interest is often pre-loaded into the MRP.
Pattern 03
Hidden fee blindness
Processing fees appear only at the final payment screen. By then, users feel committed and rarely go back.
Core insight
Trust is broken at the end, not the beginning
Users do not feel deceived when they select an EMI. They feel deceived when they see the final bill. That is a design failure.
User Scenario

The moment trust breaks

Priya, 26 — Software Engineer, Bengaluru
Buying a phone on a shopping app
Current experience

Sees 749 per month x 12 prominently. Taps proceed. Reaches payment screen. Sees 9,588 total plus 299 processing fee. Feels tricked. Completes purchase anyway but leaves a 2-star review.

Redesigned experience

Sees 749 per month x 12 equals 8,988 total upfront. Understands the 299 processing fee before proceeding. Makes an informed choice. Completes purchase with confidence.

Design Decisions

What I changed and why

Rejected
Show only monthly amount
Optimises for conversion but creates post-purchase regret. Breaks trust at the worst moment.
Chosen
Show monthly and total cost together, always
Both numbers visible at the same time. Users can make a real comparison. Trust is built before commitment, not broken after.
Rejected
Reveal processing fee only at payment screen
Feels like a bait-and-switch. Increases abandonment and negative sentiment even when users complete the purchase.
Chosen
Show all fees inline during EMI selection
Processing fee shown as a line item during plan selection. No surprises. Users who proceed have genuinely consented.
Rejected
Use No Cost EMI label without explanation
Misleading for 41% of users. Creates false expectations that damage trust when the reality is discovered.
Chosen
Explain No Cost EMI with a one-line tooltip
Interest is included in the product price. A single honest line that sets the right expectation without killing conversion.

Risks and Tradeoffs

What this design gives up

01
Short-term conversion may dip
Showing the full cost upfront may reduce impulse EMI selections. The bet is that higher-quality conversions and lower return rates offset this over time.
02
More information means more cognitive load
Showing monthly plus total plus fees adds visual complexity. Mitigated through progressive disclosure — key numbers prominent, details available on tap.
03
Business stakeholder resistance
Finance teams may push back on surfacing total cost prominently. The counter-argument: trust drives repeat purchase, and repeat purchase drives LTV.

Next Steps

Where this goes from here

1
A/B test total cost visibility
Run a controlled experiment comparing current flow vs redesigned flow on conversion rate, return rate, and NPS.
2
EMI comparison tool
Let users compare 3-month vs 6-month vs 12-month plans side by side with total cost clearly shown for each.
3
Financial health nudges
Contextual prompts like "This EMI is 18% of your estimated monthly income" — opt-in, non-judgmental, genuinely useful.
Reflection

What this taught me

The most interesting design problems are not about making things look better. They are about making things work honestly. EMI blindness exists because it is profitable in the short term. Designing against it means arguing that trust is a better long-term asset than conversion rate.

This project also taught me that edge cases are where design maturity shows. Anyone can design the happy path. The real work is in the bank eligibility failure, the coupon applied mid-flow, the user who changes their mind at the last screen.
