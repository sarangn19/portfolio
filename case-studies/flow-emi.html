Flow — Designing Against EMI Blindness

Your bank balance tells you how much money you have. It doesn’t tell you when you’re broke. Flow looks forward — projecting your balance across time so you can see exactly when it runs out, before it does.

The Problem

A number that lies

Open your bank app. You see ₹22,000. Most people read that as “I have ₹22,000 to spend.” They don’t. That number ignores EMIs due this week, subscriptions debiting Friday, the slow daily bleed of UPI payments. The balance is a snapshot of right now — not a picture of what’s coming.

By month’s end, many users are stressed and confused — not because they overspent dramatically, but because they never saw it coming. The balance was a false confidence signal the whole time.

“If I see ₹20k, I assume I’m fine. Then I check again on the 28th and wonder where everything went.”

Research participant, 26, software engineer

This isn’t a discipline failure. It’s a visibility failure. The information exists — in transaction history, SMS inbox, recurring payments. It just isn’t assembled anywhere.

Surveys confirmed scale. Thirty interviews revealed texture — including the parts that complicated the clean narrative.

01
Balance as a proxy for safety
Users don’t actively ignore future debits — they just lack a mental model that accounts for them. The balance feels authoritative; obligations feel abstract.
02
EMI decisions made in isolation
“The EMI is only ₹3,000” sounds manageable until you’re carrying five. Users evaluate each individually, almost never against total committed monthly outflow.
03
The invisible spend problem
UPI made small transactions frictionless — and therefore forgettable. Users consistently underestimated daily spend by 30–40%. Transactions aren’t hidden; they just don’t form a memory.
04
The uncomfortable one — some users knowingly ignore EMIs
About a quarter of interviewees knew their situation and chose not to engage. “I’ll adjust at the end of the month.” For them, the problem isn’t visibility — it’s avoidance. Flow isn’t designed for them. That’s a deliberate scope decision.

Three interviewees felt detailed tracking creates anxiety rather than reducing it — that not knowing the exact number is a coping mechanism. This shaped the notification approach: Flow surfaces information passively rather than alerting constantly.

Design Exploration

What we tried, what failed, and why

The first direction was a “safe-to-spend” number: subtract committed future expenses from balance, surface one figure. It tested well in concept. It fell apart in practice.

Dead end
Safe-to-spend number
When EMIs clustered mid-month, the number swung wildly. When salary was three days out, it looked falsely dire. Worst: users anchored on it as permission to spend, not as a warning. A single number can’t represent a time-based problem — it collapses the dimension that actually matters.
Rejected
Push alerts — proactive warnings
Users muted them within two weeks. Alert systems require perfect calibration — one false positive destroys credibility.
Rejected
Calendar view — financial calendar
Shows events. Doesn’t show trajectory. Tells you when the EMI leaves; not what balance looks like the day after.
Rejected
AI risk score — predictive health score
Black-box numbers don’t explain themselves. Users distrust scores they can’t verify — especially for something as personal as money.
Chosen
Balance timeline — time-based visualisation
Balance as a continuous line. Events plotted as they occur. Users see exactly when and why balance moves — and where it crosses zero. No interpretation required. Financial risk isn’t about how much money you have. It’s about when it leaves.
The Solution

One moment. One question.

Flow is used at one critical moment: before committing to a financial decision. Not as a daily dashboard. The primary trigger is one question — “Can I take on this EMI?” — and the timeline answers it with evidence rather than a guess.

The interface is a forward-looking timeline. Income arrives, expenses leave, balance moves. The point where the line crosses zero is the primary signal. Users don’t calculate anything. They just see it.

Rahul, 27 — Product Manager · ₹65,000/month · 2 existing EMIs
Sees a laptop on EMI — ₹4,200/month. Bank balance shows ₹23,000. He’s already mentally decided to take it.
Without Flow

Takes the EMI today. Hits a cash crunch on the 18th. Reaches for a credit card. The laptop ends up costing more than the sticker price.

With Flow

Opens Flow, adds the hypothetical EMI. Timeline reruns. Balance crosses zero on the 18th — four days before salary. Waits until next month when one EMI closes. Same laptop, no cash crunch, no interest, no stress.

System Logic

How it actually works

The UI is only as trustworthy as the model behind it.

EMI
EMI detection
SMS parsing identifies recurring bank debits. Users manually confirm on first occurrence; auto-detects after two consistent cycles. Irregular payments flag for review — never silently update the model.
Bills
Recurring bills
Requires three months of consistent UPI pattern before classifying something as recurring. One-time large payments that repeat don’t get auto-classified — a common false-positive source in earlier builds.
Spend
Daily spend
Rolling 30-day average, excluding EMIs and known bills. A projection, not a prediction — estimation basis is visible and overridable. Per-transaction display was tested and dropped: it caused users to lose sight of the aggregate.
Sim
Simulation
Balance recalculates after each plotted event, not just month-end. This makes the critical moment locatable — you see the specific payment that triggers a negative balance, not just that it goes negative somewhere.
Trust
Trust model
Confirmed events (salary, known EMIs) are visually distinguished from estimates. Confidence degrades toward month-end — communicated through line weight on the timeline, not fine print.

Product Risks

What could actually kill this

01
Users don’t trust projections — and churn silently
If the daily spend estimate is visibly wrong two weeks in a row, users don’t complain. They just stop checking. Trust in a financial tool is binary: once lost, it doesn’t recover. The whole product depends on projection accuracy feeling reliable in the first month.
02
Data permissions drop-off kills activation
SMS access, UPI history, and account linking are all required. Any one denied at onboarding degrades the experience. On iOS, SMS parsing isn’t available — which makes the standalone product Android-first by necessity, not choice.
03
The timeline is cognitively heavier than a number
Two users said the interface felt “like work” compared to just checking their bank app. If understanding the timeline costs more effort than ignoring the problem, users will ignore the problem. This is the core UX risk — unresolved until tested at much larger scale.

Product Positioning

What Flow is — and the deployment tradeoff

Not this
Expense tracker
Trackers explain the past. Flow projects the future. Different temporal orientation, different use case.
Not this
Budget planner
Planners require consistent input. Flow works passively — value without new habits.
This
Decision support
Used at one moment: before committing to a financial obligation. One question, one evidence-backed answer.
Option A — Bank integration
Embedded in an existing app
Transaction data exists. Trust established. No cold-start problem. Requires a bank partnership — banks historically avoid surfacing alarming projections.
Option B — Standalone
Independent product
Full design control. Fast iteration. Captures multi-bank users. Cold-start data problem. iOS SMS unavailable — Android-first by necessity.

The sequencing decision: start standalone to prove the concept, design for eventual bank API integration as the data quality upgrade. Not competing paths — sequential ones.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Flow solves ignorance, not discipline

Visibility is the precondition for better decisions — it isn’t the decision itself. The quarter of users who knew their situation and chose not to engage don’t need a better interface. They need a different relationship with money, and that isn’t a design problem.

This product is built for people who would make different choices if they could see what was coming. That group is large, real, and underserved. It is not everyone — and pretending otherwise would make the product worse for the people it can actually help.

What This Proves & What Comes Next

Demonstrated. Not yet proven.

Demonstrated
Time-based framing is more actionable than a summary number · Users locate a financial problem rather than just knowing one exists · Interface communicates without calculation
Not yet proven
Whether this changes actual decisions · Whether users return at the critical moment · Whether spend estimate accuracy degrades trust over weeks of use
01
Design the EMI decision moment as a dedicated flow
The ambient timeline and decision simulator are two products sharing one interface. They need to be separated — or one cut.
02
Expand testing across income profiles
Current sample skews salaried, urban, single bank account. Irregular income and multi-account cases need dedicated testing.
03
Run a behaviour change study
60 days. Split group. Half with Flow, half without. Compare EMI commitment rates and end-of-month balance outcomes. That’s the only data that proves the thesis.
